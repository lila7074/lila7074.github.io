Fabrics can be woven from many types of fibers, with even more options available since the industrial revolution. While many synthetic fabrics dominate the market, many consumers are beginning to seek out naturally sourced options, both for comfort and sustainability. Here we will explore the popular natural fabrics and their pros and cons in use.
WOOL
Wool is a natural fiber that primarily comes from sheep. Australian Merino sheep's wool is widely considered to be the best, but fibers are also sourced from rabbits (angora), goats (cashmere and mohair), llamas and alpacas, and even camels. No animals need be harmed for wool to be harvested. Wool can be reprocess and reused/ rewoven, sometimes mixed with other fibers, making it more sustainable.
Properties:
  • Comfortable to wear in all climates, wool is available in many weights and weaves
  • Warm in winter and cool in summer, wool breathes with the body and allows for airflow
  • Absorbs moisture better than other natural fibers, and will absorb up to 30% of its weight before it even feels wet
  • Flame-resistant
  • Somewhat crease-resistant
  • Ideal to tailor as wool is easy to shape with a steamer
  • Often blended with other fibers to reduce cost as pure wool, especially from certain animals, can be very expensive.
  • Felts if exposed to excessive pressure, heat, and/or moisture.
  • Will be bleached by prolonged exposure to sunlight
  • Can be damaged by moths as some species feed on the fibers, however proper storage and prevention is possible
COTTON
Cotton is one of the most popular and versatile of the fabrics, cotton is a natural fiber made from the fluffy seed pods, or bolls, of the cotton plant. Cotton has been in use since ancient times, with today's top cotton producers being India, the US, and several middle eastern countries. Cotton farming takes up 2.5% of the worlds arable land, and has been cultivated across the world for centuries.
Properties:
  • Absorbs moisture well and is suitable for warmer climates as it draws heat away from the body
  • Stronger wet than it is dry
  • Does not build up static electricity
  • Dyes well
  • Prone to shrinkage unless it has been treated against it
  • Will deteriorate prolonged exposure to sunlight and from mildew
  • Creases easily
  • Soils easily, but launders well
  • Odor resistant
  • Hypoallergenic properties make cotton less likely to cause skin irritation or allergic reactions
  • Easy to care for, cotton is low maintenance and durable whe properly cared for
  • Biodegradable, cotton will break down over time unlike synthetic and plastic based fibers such as polyester, making it a more sustainable choice
HEMP
Hemp fiber is a natural material from the stem of the cannabis plant. It has been used for centuries as fabric, rope, and other industrial applications. Hemp was also commonly used to make sail canvas. The word "canvas" is derived from the word cannabis. Pure hemp fabric has a similar texture to Linen. It is a sustainable fiber and is extremely durable, used today in clothes, shoes, pet gear, and more.
Properties:
  • Stronger than cotton by a significant margin, hemp is highly resistant to wear and tear
  • Less prone to shrinking, tearing, and stretching
  • Breathable, hemp allows air to circulate and moisture to evaporate, keeping the wearer cool and comfortable
  • UV resistant, Hemp naturally resists UV light, helping to protect against sun damage and prolong the fabric's lifespan
  • Antibacterial and Antimicrobial, hemp fabric has natural antibacterial and antimicrobial properties, making it a good choice for garments like socks and underwear
  • Hypoallergenic, hemp is a good choice for sensitive skin and softens with each wash it goes through
  • Sustainable both in biodegradability and farming practices, hemp plants require minimal water and pesticides during cultivation
SILK
Silk is sometimes called the queen of fabrics, and is made from the natural fibers of silkworm cocoons. Luxurious and strong, and often quite expensive, silk dates back thousands of years to its development as a fabric in China. The process of silk making was a closely guarded secret until 300 AD, and this elusive fabric is the namesake for the silk road, an ancient trade route from China to Europe.
Properties:
  • Keeps you warm in winter and cool in summer
  • Absorbs moisture and dries quickly
  • Dyes well and produces deep, rich colors
  • Fabric may cling from build op of static electricity
  • Will fade when exposed to strong prolonged sunlight
  • Prone to shrinkage
  • Best laundered by dry cleaning, silk is temperamental and should not be machine washed or dried to prevent permanent damage
  • Weaker when wet than when dry
  • May watermark from contact with water, leaving a stain on the fabric
  • Fragile compared to other natural fibers
  • Not sustainable, the cultivation of mulberry trees and the use of chemicals in the process can have environmental impacts, as well as ethical concerns for the silkworms who must die in the production process
LINEN
Linen is a natural fiber that comes from the flax plant stem. Courser than cotton, it is sometimes mixed with cotton or silk to soften the texture, however it dries faster than cotton on its own and is very strong and absorbent. Linen typically takes longer to produce than cotton, and is incredibly old, with dyed fibers found in an archaeological cave site dating back as far as 30,000 years.
Properties:
  • Cool and comfortable to wear
  • Absorbs moisture well, wicking moisture away from the body when worn
  • Very durable, linen is one of the strongest natural fibers, and is stronger than cotton
  • Shrinks when washed
  • Does not ease well
  • Tends to wrinkle and crease quite a bit
  • Prone to fraying, linen garments dont hold up well if left with raw edges and may unravel
  • Resists moths but is damaged by mildew
  • Softens with each wash despite initial stiffness
  • Biodegradable, linen will break down naturally and return to the earth
  • Abrasion resistant, linen is strong against wear and tear
  • Some temperature regulation, linen can keep you cool in the summer and slightly warm in colder temperatures, however it tends to be worn in warmer weather.
